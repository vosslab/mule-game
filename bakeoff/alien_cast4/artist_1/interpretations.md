# Artist 1 interpretations

## Design hypothesis

Brutalist Toy Box. I am an industrial toy designer who builds alien figures as if they were
injection-molded playground equipment. Each creature is assembled from a small count of large
geometric primitives (trapezoids, rounded rectangles, wedges) joined at obvious seams. At
18 to 32 px, readability relies on three deliberate constraints: (1) each creature occupies a
fundamentally different envelope shape on the canvas, (2) every ground-contact pattern is
visually distinct even as a 1 px bottom edge, and (3) frame 2 motion displaces major body
masses, not just appendages. Influences: Playmobil figures, Soviet toy design, Charley Harper
wildlife prints.

## Humanoid

The everyman: a squat, plucky biped with wide shoulders and a proud stance. I see this
character as a construction worker type, stocky, planted, ready for anything. The torso is
a tapering trapezoid giving breadth at the shoulders. Thick rectangular limbs and platform
feet give a grounded, immovable presence. Frame 2 shows a deliberate marching stride, legs
split wide, arms swinging with purpose. The face reads as focused confidence: forward-looking
oval eyes with offset pupils suggesting determination.

## Flapper

A raptor-pterodactyl hybrid, all wingspan, minimal body. I interpret this creature as a soaring
predator: thin torso slung below enormous angular wings that dominate the silhouette. The wings
are built as two large chevron polygons angled upward in frame 1 (glide) and thrust downward in
frame 2 (power stroke). A compact tail trails behind for aerodynamic balance. The face carries
an imperious hawklike stare: narrow angular eye slits angled outward, with a small gold beak
accent. The bottom third of the canvas stays empty, as this creature never lands.

## Bonzoid

Johnny 5 rebuilt as a carnival ride. I see this creature as a manic workshop robot driven by
barely-contained enthusiasm. A wide tank-tread platform anchors the bottom. Above it, a boxy
torso supports two oversized manipulator arms that extend well beyond the body width. A small
boxy head sits on a short neck, always cocked with excitement. Frame 2 throws the arms into
asymmetric positions (one up, one wide) and tilts the torso forward over the treads,
communicating urgent rolling momentum. The face has large round excited eyes, conveying manic
energy through sheer eye size.

## Gollumer

A Jabba-the-Hutt slug reimagined as a melting wax figure. Asymmetric by design: the body mass
heaps higher on one side, creating a lopsided mound that slopes to a trailing tail on the
other. No limbs, no appendages, just soft contour. The flat bottom spans the full width,
pressing against the ground like modeling clay. Frame 2 shifts the mass forward: the leading
edge extends, the trailing tail stretches and thins, communicating the ooze. Mournful drooping
oval eyes set into the upper slope give this creature its gentle, sad personality.

## Spheroid

A War-of-the-Worlds tripod miniaturized into a floating sensor probe. The dominant shape is a
large circle (the orb) occupying the upper half of the canvas. Three thin articulated legs
dangle beneath it, never touching the ground and responding to the orb's movement like a
jellyfish's tentacles. Frame 2 tilts the orb and swings all three legs to one side, showing
inertial drift. The face is two huge round startled eyes on the front of the orb, giving the
creature a perpetually surprised, curious expression. Clear air at the canvas bottom in both
frames.

## Leggite

A standing centipede rendered as a brutalist column. The body is a tall, narrow vertical ribbon
running nearly the full canvas height, with a slight sinuous curve. Short triangular legs fringe
both sides in alternating pairs, creating a corrugated edge. Frame 2 reverses the body curve
into an opposite bend and shifts the leg phase, showing the slithering undulation. Sly narrow
eyes near the top give the leggite a watchful, skittering personality. The ground contact is
many tiny leg tips, fundamentally different from any other creature's bottom edge.

## Mechtron

A shipping container on stilts. I see this creature as the most literal machine in the cast: a
wide, flat rectangular body mounted on two thick straight post-legs. No curves, no organic
shapes, pure rectilinear geometry. The body is wider than it is tall, the legs are thick and
rigid. Frame 2 steps one leg forward and tilts the body, communicating ponderous forward weight.
The gap between the two posts is clearly visible in both frames. The face is a split visor slit
running across the front of the head unit, stoic, expressionless, industrial.

## Packer

A medicine ball with feet. The packer is almost entirely its own body: an enormous oval mass
that fills the canvas, with four comically tiny T-rex legs poking out from the bottom. The body
dwarfs the limbs, it IS the cargo. Frame 1 shows the packer resting on its tiny legs, belly
just clearing the ground. Frame 2 launches it into a toad-hop: the body lifts, legs compress
and tuck, daylight opens beneath. Dopey, cheerful personality comes from small round eyes and a
wide curved smile set high on the body mass.

## Mule

A Boston Dynamics BigDog rendered as a brutalist pack robot. Horizontal body slab dominates:
long, flat, industrial. Four thin articulated legs drop below with real daylight between them
and beneath the belly. Long triangular ears point up from the front. A blunt muzzle extends
forward. A simple tail extends from the rear. Frame 2 shows a walking stride: diagonal pairs of
legs advance (front-left and back-right forward). No face, no head crop. This is a working
machine, not a character.
